Betting Z-Score: Skill or Luck?

The z-score asks one question about a betting record: how far is this win rate from what pure chance would produce over the same number of picks at the same odds?

What a z-score measures

Any coin-flip bettor will drift above and below break-even. The z-score measures how many standard deviations a record sits above (or below) the break-even expectation for its sample size. The bigger the z-score, the harder the record is to explain as luck.

It is the natural companion to win percentage and sample size: win rate says how often the bettor won, sample size says how much evidence exists, and the z-score combines both into a single "is this real?" number.

The formula

z = (W − N × p) ÷ √(N × p × (1 − p))

Where W is wins, N is decided picks (wins + losses, pushes excluded), and p is the break-even win probability implied by the odds. At the standard -110 juice, p is about 0.524; at even money it is 0.50. See average odds for how a record's typical price sets its break-even bar.

  • z around 0: indistinguishable from chance.
  • z near +2: roughly the top 2-3% of luck-only outcomes. Interesting, not proof.
  • z of +3 or more over hundreds of picks: chance alone almost never does this. This is where skill becomes the simpler explanation.

A quick example

A bettor goes 140-110 (56.0%) over 250 decided picks at roughly -110. Expected wins by chance: 250 × 0.524 = 131. Standard deviation: √(250 × 0.524 × 0.476) ≈ 7.9. So z = (140 − 131) ÷ 7.9 ≈ +1.14: a good run, but well inside what variance produces. The same 56% held over 1,000 picks pushes z past +2.2, which is a very different claim. Sample size is the whole ballgame.

Frequently asked questions

What is a z-score in sports betting?

A z-score measures how many standard deviations a betting record sits above or below the break-even expectation for its sample size and odds. It converts a win rate and a pick count into a single measure of how unlikely the record is under pure chance.

What is a good z-score for a handicapper?

Around +2 starts to be interesting and sits near the top 2-3% of luck-only outcomes. Sustained +3 or higher over hundreds of graded picks is where chance stops being a plausible explanation.

Why do pushes not count in the z-score?

A push settles at zero and provides no evidence about win skill either way, so the calculation uses decided picks only: wins plus losses.

Can a z-score be faked?

The math cannot be faked, but the inputs can be if picks are deleted or backdated. TrustMyRecord locks picks before game time with no edits or deletions, so the wins and the sample size feeding the z-score are verifiable.

Does a high z-score guarantee future profit?

No. It measures how unlikely the past record is under chance, not a guarantee about the future. Read it next to ROI, closing line value, and sample size.

The goalie decides the market before the puck drops

Everything in hockey betting starts with one question: who is in net? A confirmed starter can move a moneyline twenty cents, and a late switch to the backup reshapes the total and the puck line in the same breath. That is why a timestamp is the whole game in the NHL. The +135 you took before the goalie was announced stays +135 on your record even after the confirmation tightens it to +115, so the record proves whether you got ahead of the news or chased it once everyone else knew.

NHL bet types

Moneyline

The straight winner, and the heart of hockey betting. In a low-scoring sport, plus-money dogs cash often enough that the moneyline carries real value, and your locked average odds show whether you are paying up or getting paid.

Puck line (+/-1.5)

Hockey's fixed 1.5-goal spread. Laying -1.5 with a favorite pays a plus price but is exposed to empty-net goals that arrive too late; taking +1.5 cushions the one-goal games this sport is built on.

Totals, team totals and periods

Game and single-team goal totals, plus period and first-period lines where offered, all swung by the goalie matchup. Each settles on the relevant frame of the official result.

NHL props: skaters and goaltenders

The tracker grades both ends of the ice: skater shots on goal, points, goals and assists, and blocked shots, plus the goaltender markets, total saves and save totals, where your book lists them. Goalie save props in particular hinge on the confirmed starter, so each prop is tied to the player and number you locked and graded on the official box score.

One-goal games, empty netters and overtime

Hockey punishes a bare win-loss record harder than any sport. So many games turn on a single goal that a handicapper can run 12-4 on pure variance and regress hard the next month, and a screenshot of the hot stretch hides which is which. The tracker settles the moneyline on the final including overtime and the shootout, and totals on regulation-plus-overtime scoring, so an empty-netter that flips a puck line or a marker that pushes a total is graded the same way every time. Over a long season that consistency is what lets sample size separate a real edge from a hot run.

Frequently asked questions

What if the starting goalie changes after I lock my pick?
Your pick keeps the price and timestamp from when you logged it. Because confirmed starters can move a moneyline sharply, freezing your number before the goalie news is what keeps a hockey record honest, you cannot claim a price the backup-goalie line never offered.
Which NHL markets can I track?
Moneylines, puck lines (typically +/-1.5), game totals, team totals, and period or first-period lines where offered. Each locks before puck drop and auto-grades on the final, including overtime and the shootout where the result counts.
How are overtime and shootout results graded?
Moneylines settle on the final result including overtime and the shootout. Totals count goals scored in regulation and overtime, with the shootout adding a single goal to the winning side per standard rules, all applied automatically from the official result.
Why does hockey need more than a basic win-loss record?
The NHL is a low-scoring, one-goal sport where variance is brutal and an empty-net goal flips a puck line. A bare W/L hides whether someone is profitable or just riding variance, which is why units, ROI and a real sample size matter more here than almost anywhere.

Simulate any NFL matchup

The TrustMyRecord NFL game simulator plays out any matchup on the schedule thousands of times and returns a projected final score, a calibrated win probability, spread and total analysis, and a full reconciled box score. It is built on real drive data and current rosters, so you get a realistic range of outcomes for research, not a single confident guess.

How the NFL simulator works

Every matchup is simulated 10,000 times. Each simulation plays out a sequence of drives, and each drive's outcome is drawn from real NFL drive data rather than a smooth bell curve. That distribution is tilted by each team's opponent-adjusted points-per-drive rating, so a strong offense facing a weak defense scores more, and a good defense drags an opponent down. Because points come from real drive outcomes, the simulated margins naturally cluster on the football key numbers of 3 and 7 instead of landing on impossible or unrealistic scores.

The win probability is not raw model output. It is calibrated against a held-out set of games, so a projection of 70 percent is tuned to reflect roughly a 70 percent real hit rate. Every simulation uses a fixed seed, so the same matchup returns the same result and you can re-run it to explore how the range holds up.

What the simulator models

  • Opponent-adjusted team strength — offense and defense ratings solved jointly so a team's numbers are adjusted for the quality of who they played.
  • Home-field advantage — estimated from the data itself, not assumed as a fixed number.
  • Starting quarterback impact — measured as the difference between the expected starter and the team's baseline, so a backup starting moves the projection.
  • Injuries and inactive players — game-day inactives are removed and the box score never lists a player who is out; usage is gated to the current roster.
  • Pace and drives per game — faster teams get more possessions, which widens the scoring.
  • Turnovers and defensive scores — interceptions, fumbles, and pick-sixes are part of the drive outcomes.
  • A fully reconciled box score — passing, rushing, receiving, and scoring all add up exactly to the team totals and the final score.

How to simulate a matchup

  1. Choose a season and week at the top of the page.
  2. Tap any game in the list to run the simulation.
  3. Read the projected score, win probability, margin and total ranges, and the most likely final scores.
  4. Open the Box Score tab for full team and player statistics, and the Rosters & injuries tab to see expected starters and inactives.
  5. Re-run the same matchup to see how stable the projection is.

How to read the results and box score

The projected score is the average across all simulations, and the win probability is the calibrated share of simulations each team won. The margin and total ranges show the middle of the distribution, so you can see how wide the realistic outcomes are. The box score shown is one representative simulated game near the median: its passing, rushing, receiving, kicking, defense, and scoring summary all reconcile with the final score, so the numbers are internally consistent rather than a pile of disconnected percentages.

Limitations and responsible use

This is a model projection, not a guarantee, and not betting or financial advice. A single NFL game carries a lot of variance, so single-game margins can miss by around a touchdown and a half even when the model is well calibrated. The simulator does not yet model coaching tendencies or weather, and special-teams and penalty detail in the box score are illustrative. Use the output as one research input alongside lineups, weather, and market prices. On TrustMyRecord, simulations are a research tool, while real picks are locked before game time, auto-graded, and counted toward a public, tamper-proof record.

NFL Simulator FAQ

What is an NFL game simulator?

An NFL game simulator plays out a matchup many times using team strength, current rosters, injuries, and home field to produce a projected final score, a win probability, and a full box score. TrustMyRecord's tool simulates each game thousands of times drive by drive, so the output is a range of likely outcomes rather than a single guess.

How does the NFL simulator work?

Every matchup is simulated 10,000 times. Each simulation plays a sequence of drives whose scoring is drawn from real drive data, tilted by each team's opponent-adjusted points-per-drive rating, home-field advantage fit from data, the expected starting quarterback, and player availability. The win probability is calibrated against held-out games so it reflects real hit rates.

What factors does the NFL simulator model?

Opponent-adjusted team strength, home-field advantage estimated from data, expected starting quarterback impact, injuries and inactive players, pace and drives per game, turnovers and defensive scores, and a reconciled box score. Scoring naturally clusters on the football key numbers of 3 and 7 because points come from real drive outcomes, not a smooth curve.

Can the NFL simulator predict game results?

It produces probability estimates, not guarantees. A single NFL game has a lot of variance, so the simulator is best used as research: it shows a projected score range and a win probability you can weigh alongside lineups, weather, and market prices. It is not betting advice.

How accurate is the NFL simulator?

Across a full season of held-out games the model's calibrated win probabilities beat a coin flip on Brier score and its projected scoring tracks actual scoring closely, but single-game margins carry roughly a touchdown-and-a-half of error because real NFL outcomes are highly variable. That is why results are shown as ranges, and why locked, graded records on TrustMyRecord matter more than any projection.

Is the NFL simulator free?

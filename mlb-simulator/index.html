How It Works

Inside the TrustMyRecord MLB Simulator

What the simulator is built to do

The MLB Simulator plays each game out one plate appearance at a time. Every batter faces the selected pitcher with outcome probabilities (walk, strikeout, single, double, triple, home run, out) built from real rate stats and combined through the odds-ratio method, then runners advance through a base-and-out state machine. The box score, win probability, run distribution, strikeouts, walks, and home runs all emerge from those simulated events rather than being assigned after the fact.

Team run output is anchored to a structured expected-run model that already accounts for park, starter and bullpen quality, era context, and verified live inputs when available, so totals stay realistic while each simulated game varies the way real baseball does. It is not a guarantee; the output is a model-based estimate that should be read as scenario guidance, not certainty.

Current, classic, and mixed era modes

Current Teams mode compares modern MLB baseline profiles. Classic Teams mode uses curated historical team profiles and normalizes them into a shared simulator environment. Mixed Era Matchup lets a current club face a classic team, with era adjustment shown clearly in the projection.

When verified live context is unavailable, the simulator stays usable with baseline ratings instead of inventing unavailable injuries, weather, confirmed starters, sportsbook odds, or betting edges.

Why starter selection matters

Starting pitching is one of the biggest drivers of a baseball game projection. The starter dropdown changes both run expectation and win probability, so users can compare how different rotation options affect the matchup.

Current-team player names are loaded from the MLB Stats API active roster endpoint for the selected team, fetched with no-store behavior and a simulator build cache-buster. If that roster cannot be verified with enough hitters and pitchers for the selected team, the player box score says the roster is temporarily unavailable instead of filling rows with fallback names.

How to read the output

Use the projected score range, expected runs, win probability, and key explanation together. A higher win probability means the model sees a stronger matchup profile, not that the result is guaranteed.

For fans, it is a way to explore matchups. For handicappers, it can be one decision-making input alongside market prices, lineup news, weather, injury reports, and personal judgment.

How MLB Simulation Picks Work

A simulation pick starts with the numbers and ends with a decision. The simulator gives you a projected winner and a win probability built from team and pitcher rate stats, park, bullpen quality, and era context. A 58 percent win probability does not mean a sure thing, it means the model sees a meaningful but beatable edge. The smartest users line that estimate up against the real betting market: if the simulator likes a side more than the price implies, that is a potential edge worth a closer look.

From there a pick is a human decision, not a guarantee from a machine. You factor in confirmed lineups, weather, injury news, and your own judgment, then commit. That is exactly why TrustMyRecord keeps simulation and picks separate: the simulator is the lab, and your locked, auto-graded picks are the proof.

MLB Simulator FAQ

What is an MLB simulator?

An MLB simulator is a tool that plays out a baseball matchup or season using team and pitcher data to project outcomes. The TrustMyRecord MLB Simulator runs each game one plate appearance at a time to produce a projected winner, score range, win probability, and box score, so you can test a matchup before you bet.

How does an MLB season simulator work?

An MLB season simulator projects a full season instead of one game: division winners, wild cards, playoff brackets, and win totals. On the MLB Season Simulator you build and lock those season-long predictions, and they are timestamped and graded against real results.

Can MLB simulations help with predictions?

Yes. Simulations show a range of likely outcomes and a win probability for a matchup. They are estimates, not guarantees, so the strongest approach is to use simulation output as one input alongside lineups, weather, and market prices, then track the real result.

What is the difference between an MLB simulator and MLB betting picks?

A simulator estimates what could happen in a game or season. A betting pick is a real, locked position with a result. TrustMyRecord keeps the two separate: the simulator is a research tool, while verified picks are locked before game time, auto-graded, and counted toward your public record with units, ROI, and CLV.

Does TrustMyRecord track real betting records?

Yes. TrustMyRecord lets you lock picks before game time, auto-grades the results, and tracks units, ROI, win percentage, streaks, and CLV on a public, tamper-proof profile. Handicappers compete on transparent leaderboards where losers cannot be deleted.

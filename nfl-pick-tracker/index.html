In the NFL, the number is everything: 3 and 7

Football is the one sport built around key numbers. So many games land on 3 and 7, the margins of a field goal and a touchdown, that a half-point is the difference between cashing and pushing across a whole season. That is why a timestamped spread matters more in the NFL than anywhere: a -3 you took on Tuesday stays -3 on your record even after Sunday's steam pushes it to -3.5 through the most important number on the board. A record that cannot prove which side of the key number you were on is not a record at all.

NFL bet types

Point spreads

The headline market, where the key numbers rule. Buying or selling the half-point around 3 and 7 is a real strategic decision, and your locked line shows exactly where you stood.

Moneylines and totals

Straight winner for the underdog hunters, plus game and team totals that swing hard on weather and game script. Each grades off the final score.

First-half, quarter and alternate lines

Half and quarter spreads and totals, plus alternate spreads and totals at adjusted prices, settled on the relevant period rather than only the final.

NFL props, from the quarterback out

The NFL prop menu is wide, and the tracker grades all of it: passing yards, passing touchdowns, completions and interceptions; rushing and receiving yards, receptions and longest reception; and the touchdown markets, anytime and first scorer. Props are tied to the player and number you locked, so an inactive ruled out 90 minutes before kickoff is handled by the rules in force when you submitted, not reinterpreted afterward.

Weather, late news and beating the close

No sport runs on information like the NFL. Wednesday practice reports, Friday designations, Sunday-morning inactives and a forecast of 25 mph crosswind move spreads and totals before a snap. Sharp handicappers are judged on whether they beat the market, not just the scoreboard, and because your number is frozen first, the record captures closing line value on its own: did you take +3 before it closed +2.5, or chase it to +2? Over an 18-week season that is the entire margin, which is why sample size and a couple of lucky Sundays look so different on a locked ledger.

Frequently asked questions

Does the tracker record the line I bet or the closing line?
It records the exact spread, total or price you locked at kickoff, with a timestamp. Because that number is frozen before the market moves, your record reflects the line you actually got, which is the only fair way to judge closing line value over a season.
Which NFL markets can I track?
Point spreads, moneylines, game totals, team totals, first-half and quarter lines, alternate spreads, and player props such as passing, rushing and receiving yards, receptions and touchdowns. Each locks before kickoff and auto-grades on the final score.
Why does an 18-week sample matter so much in the NFL?
With only 17 games per team, an NFL bettor makes a fraction of the picks a baseball or basketball bettor makes. That tiny sample is exactly why a few cherry-picked winners look impressive, and why a locked, full-season record is the only honest measure.
How are timestamps protected against late injury news?
A pick is frozen the moment you log it. If a Friday or Sunday-morning inactive report moves the line afterward, your record still shows the number you took, so you cannot claim a better price than you actually had.

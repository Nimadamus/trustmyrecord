What a verified record actually proves

Before you tail a play or pay a cent for picks, you are betting on one assumption: that the seller's record is real. The pick-selling world runs on confidence, cropped slips, and an ROI nobody else can open. By the time the numbers turn out to be fiction, it is your bankroll that paid for the lesson.

A verified record turns that assumption into something you can check. It hands you the full graded history behind a handicapper's numbers, losses included, so the decision to follow them is based on their results, not their marketing.

Reading a record before you follow it

A winning slip is a story about one bet on one night. It tells you nothing about the plays sitting beside it, and it can be posted long after the result is already known. When you are deciding who gets your trust and your money, a confident thread is not due diligence.

Do the two-minute check instead: open the ledger, scroll past the highlights to the cold stretches, and confirm the picks were locked before each game. If a capper will not show a public, timestamped history, treat the missing ledger as your answer.

Verified records FAQ

What is a verified handicapper record?

A public, timestamped history of a capper's picks with odds, stakes, and graded results, so units, ROI, and win-loss can be reviewed instead of taken on trust.

How do I check a handicapper before buying picks?

Open their public profile, look for a timestamped record with both wins and losses, and confirm it cannot be edited after results are in.

Why are betting screenshots unreliable?

They can be cropped, staged, or posted after the fact, and losing picks can simply be left out. A tracked public record shows the full history, including losses.

Can I build my own verified record?

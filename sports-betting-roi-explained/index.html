Sports Betting ROI Explained

Return on investment is the cleanest measure of betting skill: net profit divided by total risked. Here is how to compute it, what it hides, and what realistic numbers look like.

Win rate impresses. ROI pays.

The formula

ROI = (net profit ÷ total amount risked) × 100

Risk 100 units across a season and finish up 5 units, and your ROI is 5 percent. Finish down 8 units and it is minus 8 percent. That is the whole formula. The power is in what it forces you to include: every bet you made, at the odds you actually took, weighted by what you actually risked. There is no way to flatter it with a hot streak or a curated highlight reel, which is exactly why serious bettors quote it and fake ones avoid it.

How to read ROI without being fooled

ROI has one weakness: small samples. A bettor can show a huge ROI over 30 picks and it means almost nothing; variance alone produces numbers like that constantly. The discipline is to read ROI together with volume. Sustained positive ROI over hundreds of graded picks is rare, because the bookmaker's margin taxes every bet. Modest-looking single digits held over real volume beats a spectacular number on a tiny sample every time.

ROI FAQ

How do you calculate sports betting ROI?

Net profit divided by total risked, times 100. Risk 100 units, profit 5 units, ROI is 5 percent.

What is a good ROI?

Sustained positive ROI over a large sample is already uncommon. Single digits over hundreds of picks is strong; huge figures usually mean tiny samples.

Why is ROI better than win rate?

Win rate ignores odds. ROI accounts for odds and amount risked, so it measures actual profitability instead of how often you cashed.

ROI vs units won?

Units is total net profit; ROI divides it by total risked so records of different volumes can be compared fairly.

From Simulation to a Smarter Read

MLB simulation predictions are only as useful as your ability to read them. When you run the MLB Simulator, you do not get a yes-or-no answer, you get a picture: a projected winner, a win probability, an expected-runs figure, and a score range. The skill is in interpreting that picture and deciding when it is telling you something the market has not priced in yet.

Reading the four numbers that matter

  • Win probability: the model's confidence, not a promise. 58 percent means a real but beatable edge.
  • Score range: the spread of likely outcomes. A tight range is a more stable read than a wide one.
  • Expected runs: the engine for totals. Compare it to the posted over/under.
  • Aggregate over 100 runs: how often a side actually wins, which smooths out single-game noise.

Simulation vs the Market

The single most valuable habit is comparing the simulator's win probability to the implied probability baked into the betting odds. If your simulation gives a team a 60 percent chance and the price only implies 52 percent, that gap is a candidate edge worth a closer look. If they agree, there is no edge to chase. This is exactly how disciplined bettors use models: not to be told what to bet, but to find disagreements worth investigating.

From there, predictions become decisions. Confirmed lineups, weather, bullpen usage, and travel all move the needle in ways a baseline simulation may not fully capture. Weigh them, commit, and then do the one thing that actually proves your read works.

What are MLB simulation predictions?

MLB simulation predictions are projections produced by running a game or season through a simulator. Instead of a single guess, you get a distribution: a projected winner, a win probability, a score range, and run totals built from team and pitcher data. They describe likely outcomes, not certainties.

Are simulation predictions accurate?

Simulations are accurate at describing ranges and tendencies, not at guaranteeing single results. Over many games a well-built model lands close to its implied probabilities, but any one game can swing on a bullpen meltdown or a weather delay. Use them as one input, not the last word.

How do I turn a simulation into a pick?

Compare the simulator's win probability to the market's implied probability from the odds. If the simulator likes a side more than the price implies, that gap is a potential edge. Then factor in confirmed lineups, weather, and injuries before you commit, and lock the pick so it counts.
